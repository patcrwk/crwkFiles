**Structural Market Gap:**
The civic intelligence market is fragmented into three separate functions—fact-checking (PolitiFact), data aggregation (GovTrack), and biographical tracking (Ballotpedia)—but none address formation quality. These tools answer "what happened?" and "who said it?" but ignore "is this designed to work?" This creates what you call the "Sarah problem": informed non-experts need structural assessment, not more information access. The market assumes civic problems stem from misinformation or polarization, missing the deeper issue of policy formation quality.

**Formation Architecture Implications:**
The gap reveals a missing layer in civic infrastructure—structural evaluation independent of political alignment. Current tools serve either fact-checkers or already-engaged citizens, leaving the broader informed public without formation assessment capabilities. The Open Record's positioning to shift conversation from "do you support this?" to "is this designed to work?" addresses an unserved structural function, but the research confirms it operates as a project, not yet the permanent institution it claims to be. The architecture must solve for institutional durability, not just analytical capability.
